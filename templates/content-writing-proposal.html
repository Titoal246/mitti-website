Why Content Writers Need a Formal Proposal

A proposal does three things: it sets clear expectations on word count, SEO requirements, and revisions; it justifies your rate with a breakdown; and it creates a paper trail if a client disputes deliverables later. Without a proposal, scope creep is nearly inevitable in content work — clients start asking for "just one more revision" or "can you also do the meta descriptions?"

India-specific note: Content writing is classified under SAC code 998313 (Online content creation services). If your annual revenue exceeds ₹20 lakh, you must charge GST. For international clients (US, UK, EU), the service is zero-rated — no GST charged, but you can claim ITC on your expenses.

How to Customise This Proposal

1. Adjust Scope for Different Content Types

The template above is optimised for blog/SEO work. For other content types, adjust section 2 (Scope of Work):

  • Website copy: Add a brand discovery questionnaire as milestone 0; list each page separately in deliverables
  • Social media: Specify platform (Instagram vs LinkedIn vs Twitter); include caption count per week and hashtag sets
  • Email sequences: List number of emails, type (welcome / nurture / promotional), and word count per email
  • Technical/SaaS: Clarify SME access — do you get 30-min interviews, product demos, or just a brief?

2. Price for Your Value, Not Your Hours

Clients who pay per word will push you to write faster and thinner. Price per article instead, and anchor to outcomes — "this article is targeting 'best CRM India' which has 12,000 monthly searches." A 2,000-word SEO article that ranks and brings 500 qualified leads per month is worth far more than ₹4/word.

3. Set Revision Expectations Upfront

The number one source of conflict in content work is unlimited revisions. The template above gives 2 rounds — enough for reasonable edits, not so many that the client rewrites the brief after delivery. If a client pushes back on the revision limit, raise your rate by 15–20% instead of removing the cap.

Red flag check: Before accepting a project, ask: does the client have a content brief / style guide? Can they give you 1–2 reference articles they like? If the answer to both is "no, you figure it out," add a ₹2,000–₹5,000 discovery fee or walk away. Vague briefs always lead to endless revisions.

  • Keyword density: 1–2% (not stuffed)
  • Meta title: under 60 characters
  • Meta description: under 155 characters
  • Internal links suggested: [X]
  • External links (authoritative sources): [X]

  • Plagiarism score: [X]% original
